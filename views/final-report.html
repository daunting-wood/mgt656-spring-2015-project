This week we continued to tweak the aesthetics of the page to put our final touches on our site. We finished all the additional user stories we wrote after completing the original requirements with the exception of adding a SQL database. This was a lofty goal and it proved to be beyond our means. We don't see any issue in not completing this story because it was not vital to the operation of the website. We instead prioritized user facing stories that would increase the usability of the site.

In additon to finising the final stories we set up Google Analytics on the page in order to see how our traffic breaks down by across diffent segments and how they flow through the site.

Here's a screen shot of our traffic breakdown

You can see most of the traffic comes from Yale SOM, but we also get significant traffic from medicine and law schools. We get a small dusting of traffic from the Divinity School.

Here's a screen shot of our flow of all traffic.

From the flow, we can see the majority of users drop off from the homepage (94.5%). Of those that click through, almost everyone goes to the event page linked from the homepage. Once they get to the event page 98.5% of users drop off before clicking another link. We can take this data to mean that we need to work on ways to increase conversions rate. Also, only 2 of over 8,600 users clicked to create a new event, so we need to work on increasing our membership.

Additionally, we ran nine days of A/B testing to determine if adding the work "RSVP" to the event title would affect click-through rate and if changing the word "donate" to "support" on the individual event page would increase click-throughs.

In order to set up the A/B testing for the RSVP link, we set up an experiment checks traffic that funnels through the home page to the /events/0 page, since the only way to get to the /events/0 page from the home page is to click the event title.

We ran an A/B testing and survey on Google Analytics testing whether using the word “donate” or “support” would garner more conversions from our users. We had 283 experiment sessions (“users”) on 5 of the 8 days of total data where there was traffic. We found that in general “support” peaked at around 20% whereas “donate” peaked around 10%. Overall the conversion rate for support was also higher on average at 3.28% vs. 2.48%. It is estimated that the variation (i.e. “support” instead of “donate”) will outperform the original by 22%. Therefore, we think that “support” leads to more conversion. We hypothesize the word “support” seems more approachable and less demanding to our users whereas “donate” is rather blunt and could apply to non-charitable causes.
